About Clinical Trials

Clinical trials are research studies using human volunteers (also called participants) that are usually intended to test whether a treatment approach, drug or a medical device is safe and effective to treat a specific disease or condition. Clinical trials produce valuable information necessary to bring a medicine to market.

Clinical Trial FAQs

Why should I consider participating in a clinical trial?

  • Clinical trials are critical in developing new tools and treatments to prevent, detect or treat diseases.

  • You may join a clinical trial for various reasons – to help improve medical care, to possibly receive the newest treatment for your illness or to have additional care and attention from healthcare professionals.

Is it safe to participate in a clinical trial?

  • Clinical trials involve risks. When weighing the risks of research, you can consider two important factors:

    • the chance of any harm occurring, and
    • the degree of harm that may result

  • Prior to joining any clinical trial, you will be provided detailed information on possible risks and benefits.

What is informed consent?

  • Informed consent is permission to be treated in the study granted by a potential trial participant after he or she has been notified of all the details of a clinical study, including possible risks and benefits.

  • The goal of the informed consent process is to make sure participants are aware of all of the aspects of taking part in the study they are agreeing to.

  • If you wish to join this clinical trial, you will receive an informed consent form that explains the study and gives you this information. Read the form carefully and be sure to ask the doctor any questions you may have.

  • If you agree to be in the study, you will need to sign the informed consent form to show that you understand the study and its possible risks.

Once I sign up, do I have to complete the study?

  • Participants may withdraw from a clinical study at any time, and for any reason, even if the study is not over.

  • If you withdraw from the trial, you may be asked to allow the medical staff at the center where you participated in the study to call you or your family to find out how you are doing afterwards.
